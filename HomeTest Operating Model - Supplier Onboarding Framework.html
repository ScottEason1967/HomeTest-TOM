The supplier-side mechanic that makes the model scale

Supplier Onboarding Framework.

A supplier is admitted to the framework once. The conformance certificate is portable across every commissioner who calls them off. The local body acts as the deployment authority, not as a re-assurer. The framework is what turns the platform's north star into a working onboarding mechanism.

Health warning · status of this page

This page is a straw man, not a finished article. It is issued to SLT for feedback and requires iteration and involvement from the programme team before it becomes anything more than that. The seven principles, five criteria dimensions, three routes and seven-stage apply journey are an early-stage map of the structure the framework could take. Almost every element on this page will need shaping, sense-checking and challenge from across the team. Treat it as a conversation starter, not a position.

The north star this delivers

Assure once. Reuse nationally.

A supplier evidences core compliance once per API they connect to. Adding a new test under an onboarded API is a lightweight check, not a full repeat. A clinical safety case, IG package and lab assurance are portable across every commissioner who calls them off. The published artefact stack carries the assurance; the local body administers the deployment, not the reassurance.

Why this exists

Four suppliers a year is not sustainable.

Supplier onboarding is a critical consideration in the commercial model. The current per-supplier assessment cannot scale to the number of conditions, configurations and call-offs the platform will need to absorb. The framework proposes onboard-once-per-API with published criteria across commercial, clinical, IG, technical and operational dimensions, tiered thresholds to keep smaller suppliers in scope, and a self-assessment-first apply journey that exposes the design to supplier challenge before NHSE engagement is required.

Seven design principles

What the framework holds itself to.

Each principle is load-bearing. The whole framework collapses if any one of them is dropped in the build.

Principle 01
Onboard once per API.
A supplier evidences core compliance once per API they connect to. Adding a new test under an onboarded API is a lightweight check, not a full repeat. Multiple APIs means one evidence pass per API, not per test.
Principle 02
Publish the criteria. Do not gate them.
Criteria live in the public domain. A supplier reads the pack, self-assesses, and decides whether to proceed before any NHSE engagement. The publication exposes the design to challenge, which improves it.
Principle 03
Self-assessment first, human review second.
Self-declared evidence against published criteria is the first step. Automated triage handles completeness. Human judgement is reserved for the grey-area calls with a specific escalation path for IG and clinical safety.
Principle 04
Tiered thresholds so the floor doesn't exclude.
A two-person biotech and an established lab both need to pass the patient-safety and data-protection floor. Other dimensions scale to volume, scope and risk. Smaller suppliers are not priced out by balance-sheet alone.
Principle 05
No defensive filtering.
Criteria exist to assess fitness, not to ration throughput. Where capacity is the real constraint, the answer is automation or tiering, not arbitrary barriers.
Principle 06
Every artefact exists once.
DPIA template, DSA template, privacy notice boilerplate, clinical safety starter, technical integration guide. One version of each, versioned centrally, reused across pathways.
Principle 07
Supplier experience is a design input.
The apply journey is designed from the supplier's perspective first; NHSE operational requirements are layered on afterwards. If a supplier cannot find the pack, cannot self-assess, or cannot apply without an NHSE-specific conversation, the design has failed.

Three access routes

Tiering reflects scope, not preference.

A supplier's tier reflects the scope they are asking to deliver, not a preference about who gets in. A Route B supplier can progress to Route A by demonstrating scaled-tier evidence through live operation. The bar isn't lowered; the access route is differentiated.

Route A · Standard
The full bar across all five dimensions.
Suppliers delivering end-to-end capability against an API must meet the scaled tier on every dimension. The route for current Beta-stage suppliers with established delivery capacity.
Route B · Light-touch
Constrained scope, core tier plus scope-relevant scaled.
For suppliers delivering a constrained scope: a single test under an existing API, a single LA pilot, a proof-of-concept integration. Core tier across all dimensions, scaled tier where material to scope. Lets smaller suppliers demonstrate fitness in practice rather than being excluded by balance-sheet alone.
Route C · Partnership
Design-and-validation partnership criteria.
For NHS provider partners and acute use case partners, where the arrangement is a design-and-validation partnership rather than a commercial supplier contract. Distinct criteria reflecting the partnership archetype. IP vests in NHSE.
Seven-stage apply journey

Stages 1 to 3 happen without NHSE engagement.

A supplier can decide not to proceed without ever entering the system. NHSE engagement begins at stage 4. The orange-bordered stages are the first NHSE touch points.

1
Discovery
Supplier finds the criteria pack via Future NHS, public HomeTest and HealthStore pages, Find a Tender or partnerships outreach.
2
Self-assessment
Supplier works through the published checklist using the boilerplate artefacts. Self-declared position against each criterion.
3
Expression of interest
Supplier submits a structured EOI declaring the API(s), the scope, the self-assessment result by criterion and the evidence references.
4
Automated triage
System checks completeness, valid evidence references, declared passes at core tier. Specific feedback on bounce-backs.NHSE engages here
5
Human review
Workstream leads review by dimension. Commercial, IG, clinical safety, technical. Time-boxed with a committed SLA. Grey-area judgements escalate.NHSE review
6
Onboarding proper
API integration, test-suite conformance, live-like testing, go-live sign-off. The heavy lift. Triage upstream ensures effort here is spent on integration not vetting.NHSE delivery
7
Per-test and per-scope additions
Once a supplier is onboarded to an API, adding a new test or expanding into a new LA scope is a lightweight check plus a configuration change. Annual refresh default; earlier on material change, incident or regulatory shift.
Four open decisions for SLT

Decisions that are load-bearing for the commercial model.

Open Q for SLT
Endorse the compelled-removal protocol. Removal from the approved list is the only enforcement leverage NHSE holds in the interim model. No framework call-off and therefore no termination clause. The protocol must be defensible the first time it bites: clear failure thresholds, proportional escalation, evidence-led decision-making, named removal authority, supplier right of appeal, commissioner notification, full audit trail. SLT endorsement needed before the protocol can be relied on. Decision sits with SLT with Commercial Operations and NHSE Commercial and NHS Legal. See on Status →

Each carries a working assumption to flush out direction. None of them are optional detail.

Decision 01
Framework ownership and assurance-running.
Who owns the framework and who runs the operational assurance. Options: NHSE direct, Shared Business Services, Crown Commercial Service, third-party delivery partner. Each carries different cost, control and accountability profiles.
Working assumption. NHSE direct for the framework itself to keep strategic control at the centre; a delivery partner (SBS-shaped, not necessarily SBS) for operational assurance to scale capacity without internal headcount.
Decision 02
Commercial criteria owner.
The commercial dimension is the most under-developed of the five. It needs a named owner to produce a first draft for internal review.
Working assumption. Future-state workstream picks this up post initial discovery, with SLT direction.
Decision 03
Tiered threshold position from Year 1.
Whether the light-touch route (Route B) operates from Year 1 or is added later once the standard route is running.
Working assumption. From day one. Without it, Year 1 signals the framework is incumbent-only, which contradicts the equity framing and loses a market of smaller labs and innovators at the exact point we need them engaged.
Decision 04
Apply journey mechanism.
Atamis handles formal procurement cleanly. It is less comfortable as the home for EOI-stage partnership routes which are lighter-touch and not bound to a procurement timeline.
Working assumption. Atamis for formal procurement; a dedicated EOI form for partnership routes in Year 1; migrate to an integrated portal when pathway-level self-service goes live October 2026.

The reason this exists

Why.

The operating model exists so anyone can test at home, get a clear result, and take action with confidence: whether they self-screened with the NHS App (Abdul, CVKM), a clinician requested it (Mark, PSA-AS) or their GP arranged it as routine monitoring (Layla, HbA1c). Three lenses explain what that means in practice: the problem we're solving, the economics that have to hold, and the reasons different audiences engage.

Lens 01
Purpose
Why we're doing this. Four drivers, equally weighted: better access to testing for people who can't easily reach a clinic; earlier detection through screening at population scale; fairer outcomes by closing gaps in who gets tested and when; and system efficiency by easing pressure on primary care. Keyed to the DDTx 26/27 programme outcomes: volumes, equity, action taken on results.
Lens 02
Economics
The commercial logic. Suppliers must make money. Commissioners must save money or get more for the same money. The centre is funded sustainably. If any of these fails, the platform fails.
Lens 03
Adoption
Why each audience engages. Patient: privacy, convenience, dignity, access. Commissioner: easier than bespoke. Supplier: clean route to volume. Clinician: pathway fit. Centre: strategic role.
What we're trying to figure out at a strategic level

Three strategic questions.

Cross-cutting questions that apply to every product, not just to one stage or one pathway. The three lenses above frame why the model exists. These three frame what is genuinely hard about making it work.

Question 01
Route to market
What is the best route to market? How does the centre create a market for each product so that demand and supply meet in a way that scales?
Question 02
Commercial viability
How is this made attractive to commissioners, financially viable for suppliers, and demonstrably value for money for the NHS, all at the same time?
Question 03
Supply chain at scale
How is a supply chain built that can handle different use cases, different initiation patterns, and grow with volume without fragmenting?
The shape of the model is not new

Same shape as existing NHS national models.

The model is not inventing a new way of doing things. It is taking proven NHS national mechanics and applying them to home testing. Accreditation sits with established bodies that already do it: CQC for provider quality, the National Cancer Board, GIRFT or the relevant Royal College depending on the test. Listing works the way the National Choice Framework already lists eligible providers for elective care. Access works the way ERS already gives clinicians a national digital route to those providers. NHSE is the platform that brings the three together for home testing; it is not the body that decides whether a test is clinically safe. That sits where it sits today. Same shape, new application.

Why each player engages

Three actors. One platform around them.

Patient, commissioner and supplier each have different reasons to engage. They overlap with each other in specific pairs, and they all share one centre: the service has to actually work, safely, and to be trusted. NHSE / HomeTest is the platform that holds it all together. Not a fourth player: the environment in which the three relate. Aisha, David and Ngozi are part of that platform: Aisha IS the clinical framework, David IS the commercial framework, Ngozi IS the information governance framework. The platform is the sum of those three layers, not a separate thing alongside them.

The centre is the most important region.
The smallest overlap is the most powerful one. If the service doesn't actually work, if results aren't trusted, if it isn't clinically safe, none of the other regions matter. Everything else exists to make the centre true.
Health outcomes live between Patient & Commissioner.
This is the NHS mission expressed in pairs. Equity, access at scale, the population-level reasons commissioners spend money, the personal reasons patients use the service. Both sides of the same coin.
Economics live between Commissioner & Supplier.
Not in the patient's experience, but the foundation that lets it exist. Pricing, payment, risk, value for money. If the economics don't work for both sides, the service doesn't get bought, doesn't get built, doesn't reach the patient at all.
Aisha is part of the platform: the clinical framework.
Aisha doesn't sit inside any one circle. She is part of the NHSE/HomeTest envelope: the standards, the hazard log, the assurance bar that lets any pattern go live safely. Without her layer, the centre condition (the service works safely) cannot be true.
David is part of the platform: the commercial framework.
David doesn't sit inside any one circle either. He is part of the NHSE/HomeTest envelope: the procurement vehicle, the contract architecture, the commercial overlay that lets commissioners buy and suppliers sell at scale. The economics edge runs through his layer.
Ngozi is part of the platform: the information governance framework.
Ngozi doesn't sit inside any one circle either. She is part of the NHSE/HomeTest envelope: the DPIA, the DSA, the controller designation rules, the lawful basis position. Every data flow in the service crosses her framework. Without her layer, the service cannot lawfully process patient data at all.

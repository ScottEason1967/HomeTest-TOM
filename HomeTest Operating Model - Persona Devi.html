Devi.

Patient · Primary care initiated · HbA1c
“I've had HbA1c done every year for three years now. Each time it's meant swapping a shift and losing income for a morning. If it can come to me, it'll actually happen on time.”
47, care coordinator at a local hospice, British Nigerian, born and lived in Peckham all her life. Both her mother and father were diagnosed with type-2 diabetes in their fifties. She sits on her practice's at-risk register. Her most recent HbA1c came back at 44 mmol/mol, pre-diabetes territory, no medication yet, annual review with dietary and activity advice the current plan. She has not missed a review yet, but each one has meant re-arranging a shift, negotiating cover, and losing income for the morning. She is the equity case for the HbA1c pathway.

Devi
Why Devi is here Devi is the patient archetype for the HomeTest HbA1c configuration, the current working GP-initiated exemplar per the 26 March 2026 Programme Board Pivot Paper (HbA1c, CRP or lipid profiles named as illustrative GP-init candidates). She represents the at-risk register cohort where annual HbA1c monitoring runs at scale in primary care today. If HomeTest does not work for this monitoring cadence, the platform's headline GP-init volume case does not land. Devi sits inside Anita's commissioning shape and on Hannah's review cycle.

Why this role matters

Devi is the patient archetype for the primary-care-initiated pattern in HomeTest. Abdul is patient-initiated (he self-screens through the NHS App, CVKM check follows). Mark is clinician-initiated (his consultant requests PSA on active surveillance). Devi is GP-initiated: her GP arranges the HbA1c around her annual review. Together the three cover the SLT-confirmed initiation patterns. They also test whether the model serves access. Devi is the one whose journey to a clinic is the actual barrier — care coordinator shift work, no easy morning slot for phlebotomy, income lost every time she has to swap. If the model only works for people who can take time off, it isn't doing the equity job. Her at-risk register position means the annual HbA1c isn't optional; it's the anchor of her care plan. Home-based sampling turns the appointment barrier into a solved problem.

The GP-init archetype carries the equity load. Abdul and Mark both have care routes that don't depend on physical clinic access barriers. Devi is the one who can't easily take time off, can't easily reach the clinic, and for whom a kit at home is the difference between staying on the annual review cadence and drifting off it. Family history and ethnicity put her at a two-to-three-fold higher lifetime risk of type-2 diabetes than the base rate. The annual HbA1c is the surveillance mechanism holding that risk in view. If Devi drops off the cycle because the appointment is the barrier, the model isn't doing what the Purpose statement says it does.

What's different about Devi's journey

Devi is not being treated; she is being monitored. The clinical response to any given HbA1c is a conversation, not an intervention: lifestyle advice, risk-trend interpretation, sometimes a referral into the local diabetes prevention programme, occasionally a medication start if the trend moves the wrong way. That makes her the paradigm case for the model's result-before-consultation pattern. The value comes from the consultation being informed by fresh data, not from the phlebotomy step being clever. Kit dispatched around her review date, finger-prick sample collected at home, returned by post, result written back to the GP record ahead of her review conversation. Hannah opens the review with the number already in front of her. Devi doesn't order the test, and she doesn't need to react to the result on her own; her GP writes to her if action is needed. Annual cadence, low-friction sample method, clean GP-record write-back.

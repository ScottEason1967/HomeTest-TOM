Catherine.

Acute Trust commissioner · PSA Active Surveillance · established pre-treatment cohort
“The clinical case is settled. I need the operational shape, the tariff envelope and the contract route to hold up at audit. Once that lands, my Trust can adopt.”
Catherine is the Acute Trust commissioner who holds the budget for PSA Active Surveillance services for newly-diagnosed men on the active monitoring pathway, pre-treatment. The in-scope cohort sits inside NICE NG131 (CPG 1, sometimes CPG 2). Mark sits inside her cohort. Her institution is an acute trust under the tariff envelope, not LA budgets. Her route is the PSA-AS Monitoring Episode tariff under design (shadow 26/27, dual-run 27/28 at 50%, live 28/29 at 100%). Her sign-off depends on the contract route landing alongside Adam's clinical pathway.
Why Catherine is here Sarah was originally framed as a single universal commissioner across HIV (LA), PSA-AS (Acute Trust) and iron deficiency anaemia (ICB / GP). That collapses three different institutions doing three different jobs into one person, and it doesn't hold. Catherine is the Acute Trust commissioner persona separated out from Sarah. She is anchored on the PSA Active Surveillance established pre-treatment cohort (NICE NG131, CPG 1 sometimes CPG 2), which is Mark's cohort. Sarah remains as the LA sexual health commissioner for HIV. Ruth stays as the PSA-AS monitoring-gap commissioner (TBC). Anita is the ICB / GP commissioner for HomeTest iron deficiency anaemia.

What Catherine needs from the framework

Three things. First, a tariff envelope she can plan against: the PSA-AS Monitoring Episode tariff under design has to land with a glide-path her Trust's finance team can take to board. Second, a contract route that survives audit: acute trust commissioners commission through the Standard Contract architecture, and the call-off mechanism has to slot inside it. Third, an end-to-end clinical pathway her consultants will actually adopt, that's where Adam sits.

The commissioner pitch in 60 seconds

The pitch Catherine hears in the first ten minutes, before she decides whether the conversation is worth continuing. Direct answers to the questions she will ask. No aspiration, no architecture, no abstractions.

Where does the value land for me? Outpatient capacity released, every PSA done at home is one fewer routine appointment slot consumed, and that slot goes into elective recovery and counts toward the Trust's capacity commitments. Phlebotomy workload eases. Lab capacity is protected from competing with diagnostic tests. The Trust does not save cash on the unit cost of a test (per-test economics favour in-clinic phlebotomy on raw numbers). The value lands in released capacity at the pathway level, which is why pathway-level tariff is the funding unlock, not per-test cashable saving.
Who's going to pay me for this? No separate budget line. The PSA Active Surveillance Monitoring Episode tariff in design at NHSE Strategic Finance is the intended funding mechanism, on a glide path of shadow in 26/27, dual-run at 50% in 27/28, live at 100% in 28/29. Until the tariff lands, the working arrangement is bilateral commissioning under the NHS Standard Contract with the supplier. The Trust does not hold a contract with the platform itself: the platform provides the capability, the Trust commissions the service. The wider procurement vehicle is unresolved at SLT level and tied to the 22 June 2026 SLT Leeds playback.
How quickly can I get this in front of patients? Mobilisation is weeks not months for a Trust that opts in. The pre-mobilisation work has already been done at the lead acute partner; the pattern is reusable. Clinical readiness of the consultant urologist team is the gating factor, not the platform. Local Trust pathway changes (MDT process, EPR integration, urology team adoption) take longer than the platform stand-up.
What if it doesn't work for my cohort? Exit terms sit in the call-off contract. The Trust can revert to in-person phlebotomy at any point, the AS cohort is a watch-and-wait population by definition, so switching back to in-person testing is a clinical step-back, not a service breakdown. Switching supplier within the pattern is supported via the supplier-switching mechanism. No multi-year platform lock-in.
How is this different from what other Trusts are already running? Existing at-home PSA services are one-off pilots inside individual Trusts. Each one runs under its own commercial arm or research programme. They are not interoperable, not nationally accredited, not portable between Trusts. The platform is a national capability any Trust can plug into, same accreditation, same supplier pool, same result-routing back to local EPR. A Trust adopting it gets capacity without having to stand up its own commercial arm.

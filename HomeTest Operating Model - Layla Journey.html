Hearts and Minds · a patient journey

Layla's journey.
Monitored without the half-day off.

Layla is 42, a single parent of two school-age kids, working two part-time jobs — one of them shift-based. Iron deficiency anaemia, diagnosed three years ago, managed with ferrous sulphate. Her GP needs an FBC and ferritin every six months to check her response. Simple, routine — and the hardest test in this model to actually get done.

Built from the model's iron deficiency anaemia configuration: the GP-initiated pattern. Use Case 3, design phase. ⚑

1
The appointment she keeps missing

The barrier isn't the needle. It's the half-day.

A routine blood draw means a clinic slot in working hours, and a clinic slot in working hours means lost income from a shift-based job — plus the school run rearranged around it. So the bloods slip. Not because Layla doesn't care about her anaemia, but because the system priced the test in a currency she can't spare.

A missed FBC is recorded the same way as any other non-attendance, and an anaemia that isn't measured is an anaemia that isn't managed. Layla is the patient for whom the journey to the clinic is itself the clinical risk.

The Sean thread
Layla's wall is time; Sean's is the system itself. Different walls, same lesson: a “did not attend” code can't tell would not from could not — and the model is built for the could-nots.
2
Her GP arranges it

Nothing for Layla to order, chase or remember.

Layla doesn't order her own test, and no hospital consultant does either. Her GP arranges it from the practice system as part of the standard anaemia review cycle — the same cycle the practice already runs, with the kit replacing the clinic slot. No catalogue, no app demanded of her, no new behaviour to learn. The review cycle stays Hannah's; only the venue changes.

Her terms
Patient route choice preserved
The clinic stays available; the kit just removes the half-day price tag the clinic was charging her.
GP-initiated · practice system · routine review cycle
3
A Saturday, not a sick day

The kit comes to her. The result goes to the record.

The kit arrives at the house. Layla takes the sample around her shifts — a Saturday morning at the kitchen table — and posts it back. The FBC and ferritin results land in her GP record as part of the standard review, and she is contacted only if something needs action. No news, handled properly, is the cycle completing.

“I can't take half a day off for bloods. The kit comes to me, I do it on a Saturday, the GP sees the result.”
Layla, 42 — on what access actually means

Why her journey carries the most weight
The equity load, and the volume case.
Primary-care monitoring is the largest pool of long-term-condition testing in the NHS. If HomeTest doesn't work for the anaemia review cycle, the platform's headline volume case doesn't land — and if it only works for people who can take time off, it isn't doing the equity job. The Purpose statement — better access, earlier detection, fairer outcomes — reads true exactly when Layla's journey works. She is the test of whether the model does what it says on the tin.
4
The cycle that fits her life

Six months later, it simply happens again.

The next review falls due; the practice system raises it; the kit arrives; the Saturday morning does its work. Layla stays in work, her kids' routine stays whole, and her anaemia stays measured — monitored without ever again choosing between her health and a shift. If her ferritin drifts, Hannah sees it in the record and the contact happens because it should, not because a slot finally aligned.

Status, honestly: this configuration is Use Case 3 on the Year-1 list, in design phase. Pattern picks are working assumptions and several entries genuinely say TBC. The journey above is what the design is being built to deliver. ⚑

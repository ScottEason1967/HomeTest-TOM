Adam.

Consultant urologist · clinician-initiated patterns · PSA Active Surveillance lead case
“I do not need a new tool. I need the patients I cannot see in clinic to stay in surveillance. Give me a reliable way to monitor them at home and put the result back in front of me, and I will use it.”
Adam is a consultant urologist at a large acute trust running a PSA Active Surveillance cohort. He holds clinical accountability for the men on monitoring. Today he sees them in clinic on a quarterly cycle. Some miss appointments because of work or travel. Some drop out of surveillance entirely. The drop-outs are the patients he worries about most.
Where Adam sits Adam is the consultant urologist at the heart of the clinician-initiated, patient-confirmed pattern. He is the live case for clinician-initiated configurations on HomeTest, anchored to PSA Active Surveillance. Other clinician-initiated configurations follow as they mature (HbA1c GP-side, pharmacy-initiated as a working sketch).

What drives him

The patients he can't see are the patients he loses. The discovery research has surfaced consistent clinician-side language across acute pathways. Patients who live far from the clinic, who work shifts, who don't drive, who care for someone at home: these are the patients who miss appointments and slowly drop out of surveillance. The clinician sees this happening and cannot fix it with a clinic-only model. The motivation for clinician-initiated home testing comes from there, not from technology preference.

The clinical role is preserved. Adam is not interested in a model that quietly removes him from the loop or sends raw numbers to patients without clinical interpretation. The result comes back to him first. He reads it against the PSA trajectory he is tracking. He decides what happens next. The model that earns clinician adoption is the one that protects clinical accountability rather than routing around it.

Capacity for the patients who need to be seen. Every patient whose quarterly bloods can be done at home is a clinic slot freed for someone who clinically needs to be in the room. Adam is operating against a clinic that is already over-booked. Home testing is a way to reach more patients without adding more clinic time he doesn't have.

Three drivers

Reach
Patients the in-clinic service misses: rural patients with travel barriers, patients with practical attendance constraints (work pattern, caring responsibility), patients who stop attending after a few cycles. Surveillance attrition is a recognised clinical problem.
Adherence
A kit at home does not get missed because of work, weather, transport or childcare. The model reduces the friction that drives drop-out in monitoring pathways where dropping out is the clinical risk.
Clinic capacity
Routine quarterly monitoring done at home frees the slot for the patient who needs to be seen in person. The pattern does not displace the clinical role; it changes which patients reach the clinic and why.

Why this role matters

Clinician-initiated configurations only work if the clinician chooses to initiate. The model can be designed, contracted, accredited and assured, and still fail if the consultant on the ward decides not to use it. Adam is the adoption test. The shape of the pattern, the workflow integration, the result-return route, the route-choice conversation: all of it has to land in a way that makes his job easier rather than adding admin he doesn't have time for.

Patient route choice is preserved. Adam sets the intent to order; the patient confirms the order at every cycle and retains the choice between in-person and home test. This is not the clinician forcing a delivery mode on the patient. It is the clinician offering a delivery mode the patient can accept or decline. The consent shape mirrors the active surveillance trial consent pattern.

What Adam needs from the platform

Three things, in order. First, the result has to come back to him with the clinical context (the patient's PSA trajectory, prior results, time since last reading), not as a number floating without history. Second, the recall and scheduling has to ride underneath whatever tracker his trust already uses, not replace it. Third, no extra admin on his clinic time: ordering has to be lightweight, alerting has to surface only what needs his attention, and the patient's confirmation step has to be the patient's, not his.

Open questions
  1. What changes in Adam's week-to-week workflow when home testing is the default for a portion of his cohort.
  2. Where the result actually surfaces in his trust system and how he is alerted to it.
  3. How recall and scheduling integrates with the trust's existing tracker system without duplicating cadence management.
  4. How the route-choice conversation with the patient gets done at first order and at every repeat order.
  5. How the post-result patient contact happens when the result is abnormal, and who owns that contact in his service.
  6. How clinical-research consent and active-surveillance trial protocols interact with the home-testing route at the cohort level.
Status: Adam is a sketch grounded in qualitative discovery research, not a validated persona. The benefit framing draws on clinical conversations across active surveillance, sexual health, and primary-care pathways. The open questions are the items the configuration design work has to land before HomeTest moves out of the prospective phase into a live clinician-initiated deployment.

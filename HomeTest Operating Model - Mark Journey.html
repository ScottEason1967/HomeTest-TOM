Mark's journey.
The phone that doesn't ring.

Mark is 65, a retired teacher, and lives with his wife. Last year he was diagnosed with low-grade prostate cancer (CPG 2) and chose Active Surveillance — active monitoring rather than immediate treatment, per NICE NG131. He sees his consultant urologist every six months. Between those visits, his PSA is what stands watch.

1
The diagnosis he lives with

Watching, not fighting. That was the choice.

Low-grade prostate cancer grows slowly, and for men like Mark the evidence says watch it carefully rather than treat it immediately — treatment has costs of its own. Active Surveillance means sustained vigilance: regular PSA tests, watching the trend for any rise that would change the plan. The test is routine. What it stands guard over is not.

The old shape of that vigilance: a trip to hospital phlebotomy for every routine draw — car park, waiting room, needle, journey home — for a test whose most likely outcome is “no change”. Multiply that by every man on a surveillance list, for years, and the clinic is full of people who didn't need to be there.

2
His consultant orders

Adam sets the intent. Mark confirms it.

Mark never sees a catalogue, never picks a supplier, never holds a contract. His consultant urologist identifies him from the active surveillance list and sets the intent to order. Mark confirms the order, the address and the timing — and that confirmation step repeats at every cycle, so the test never arrives unrequested or unexplained.

The choice stays his. At the point of confirmation, Mark can still choose the in-person route — the home kit is an offer, not a conscription. He takes the kit.

3
Kit out, sample back

A capillary draw at his kitchen table.

The kit arrives at the house — a capillary draw, not a full venous one. Mark takes the sample at home and returns it by Royal Mail to a specialist pathology lab. The result goes to his consultant first, reviewed in trend against every previous reading — not as a number in isolation, but as a line that is either flat or isn't.

“The test comes to me. The consultant sees the result. They only call me if something needs to change.”

Between the cycles
The silence is the service.
For a man on lifelong surveillance, the phone that doesn't ring is the whole point. No news doesn't mean nobody looked — it means somebody looked, in trend, against everything that came before, and the line is flat. Mark's retirement is not scheduled around phlebotomy appointments, and his cancer is watched more closely than it ever was in the waiting-room era. The result he doesn't hear about is the model working.
4
Living on surveillance

The cycle repeats. The list stays whole.

Every cycle runs the same way: intent from the surveillance list, confirmation from Mark, kit, sample, lab, trend review. Where a trust already runs a condition-specific tracker for scheduling and recall, HomeTest sits underneath it as the remote-delivery layer — the tracker stays the source of truth for cadence and clinical management. If Mark's PSA ever rises, escalation starts from a trend the team can see whole, and the clinic slot goes to the conversation that needs it.

And beside Mark's cohort sit two more: a radiotherapy follow-up cohort (men in post-treatment PSA surveillance, watched for biochemical recurrence — a different clinical state from Active Surveillance, served by the same machinery), and a quieter one: men referred to Urology with elevated PSA but no cancer diagnosis, who currently fall between acute care and a GP contract that carries no obligation to monitor them. The same kit-and-trend machinery is the candidate answer for that monitoring gap. ⚑ prospective cohort, commissioning shape held open.

For Mark
Surveillance without the waiting room.
Routine monitoring happens at his kitchen table, on his calendar. He confirms every order, keeps the in-person option, and hears from his team when it matters — which is the only time anyone wants that call.
For the care team
Trend lines, not snapshots.
Adam's surveillance list runs itself between escalations: results return in trend, recall stays with the existing tracker, and outpatient phlebotomy stops absorbing men whose most likely result is “no change”. Clinic time concentrates on the patients whose lines moved.
For the system
A surveillance pathway that scales.
Lifelong cohorts grow; clinic capacity doesn't. Remote PSA monitoring moves the routine load to kit-and-post logistics and a specialist lab — and offers the monitoring-gap cohort a way to be monitored at all, rather than falling between two contracts.

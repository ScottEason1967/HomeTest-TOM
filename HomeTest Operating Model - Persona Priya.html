The cast · meet Priya

Priya.

NHSE Platform
“We don't deliver care. We don't sign local contracts. We build the rails everyone else runs on.”
Senior product manager at NHS England Digital. Builds and runs the platform Abdul orders through. Cares about NHS App integration, NHS Login, NHS Notify, and the platform fee model that makes the platform self-sustaining.

Why this role matters

The platform is the connective tissue. Priya keeps it standing: the catalogue, the routing, the integration with NHS App, the supplier APIs, the MI dashboards, the identity verification, the comms layer. None of the other roles can do their job if the platform isn't there. She's also the one carrying the long-term funding model: the per-transaction fee mechanism that lets the platform sustain itself without permanent central subsidy.

The platform is invisible when it works and blamed when it doesn't. A successful month for Priya is one where Sarah didn't notice the platform existed, Joe got his MI on time, and Abdul, Mark and Layla all received their results without anyone calling support. Platform success metrics are the absence of incidents, not the presence of features.

What drives her

She moved deliberately from private-sector tech to NHS. Priya spent eight years at a major consumer-platform company before joining NHS England Digital. The move was a conscious downshift on pay and an upshift on purpose. She wanted to apply the discipline she'd learned building platforms at scale to a service that mattered , one where reliability and reach were the same thing as outcomes.

A family experience that anchored the move. Her father died of a stroke when she was at university. The clinical response was good. The system response around it was fragmented across multiple NHS organisations that didn't share data effectively. She watched her mother re-explain her father's history four times to four different teams. The memory is what she points at when colleagues ask why she left commercial tech.

What it adds up to. She knows what consumer platforms can do at scale because she's built them. She knows what NHS could do at the same scale because she's seen the inverse. Her job is to make the Digital Therapeutics platform as boring and reliable as a well-run tech-company platform , predictable, standards-based, properly maintained. Boring at platform level is how care becomes excellent at patient level.

What Priya owns

Five things sit explicitly under her stewardship. First, the catalogue: what's available, who's accredited, what the criteria are. Second, the integration foundation: NHS App, NHS Login, NHS Notify, supplier APIs, FHIR observation routing. Third, the supplier onboarding gateway: the seven-stage assurance process every new supplier passes through. Fourth, the MI and data flow integrity: foundation-level reporting that lets every other role see what's happening. Fifth, the platform's funding model: the percent fee per transaction that keeps the lights on without a permanent subsidy.

Open Q: BAU funding for the national orchestration layer. The percent-fee-per-transaction model covers the platform foundation at steady state. What it doesn't yet size is the BAU team behind it: routing, DevOps, interoperability, assurance management, Blue Book maintenance, supplier onboarding, analytics, standards updates. The headcount, the salary envelope, the operational running cost. Owner: Commercial Operations with NHSE Commercial; Health Economist briefed to model BAU cost. Surfaced on the WHO page Open Governance Questions panel.

Live work in flight on the platform

Four pieces of platform work are in flight today and sit directly on Priya. Each one has a position that is moving and affects how the platform is shaped at go-live.

Test catalog as assurance and discoverability layer (not a marketplace)
The test catalog is what lets commissioners and clinicians find a test and trust it has been admitted. Supported tests, providers, pathways, with the SNOMED codes that make them addressable in the wider NHS data estate. It is not a marketplace. There is no purchase or transact function inside it; buying happens through the framework David runs. Principles to be ratified at programme level; gap analysis against the FDP genomics catalog still outstanding.
NHS App test-results integration
Working partnership with the NHS App test-results team on native rendering of HomeTest results in the App. Interim pattern is a web hand-off into a HomeTest-hosted results UI; the native render is the target. Scope in flight; the App team owns its own roadmap, HomeTest aligns to it.
PDM as the long-term canonical record of test results
The longer-term target for test results is the Personal Data Model, with the NHS App rendering against it natively. Supersedes the interim web hand-off pattern over time and replaces the IM1 aggregator pattern for HomeTest results. The platform integration is Priya's; the canonical data model alignment is Aisha's. Both have to land for the target to be reachable without rework.
Open Q for SLT: platform funding model in steady state
Working position is a small percent fee per transaction that keeps the platform sustaining itself without permanent central subsidy. This is an architectural commitment, not just a pricing question: it is the answer to "who pays for the centre once Build ends". Not yet endorsed at SLT level and not yet defended at NHSE Strat Finance. Without it, the platform's BAU funding sits exposed once discovery wraps.

BAU and DevOps: what stays running after discovery

The platform doesn't stop evolving once discovery ends. Two things sit alongside the BAU team and need to be defined now, not later: the DevOps model that keeps the platform iterating in flight, and the catalogue evolution function that handles new test types, new supplier phenotypes (capillary, transdermal, microsampling), and new assurance categories as they emerge. The interoperability layer for ordering and resulting will change over time as standards mature, the National Diagnostic Service evolves, and EPR integration patterns settle. None of this is static. Sizing the BAU team in isolation undersells what the platform needs to keep doing.

Timing matters here. Sourcing a DevOps capability through a framework, writing the business cases, and getting approvals to flow has a lead time measured in months not weeks. If the Year 2 platform is to land on time, the BAU + DevOps sizing has to be done in parallel with the operating model itself, not after it.

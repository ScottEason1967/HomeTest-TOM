Joe.

Supplier
Read Joe's journey → Open the framework →
“I'll join the framework if the criteria are clear, the onboarding is light, and I can make a margin per test.”
Operations director at a HomeTest supplier. Runs kit fulfilment, lab analysis, return logistics. Cares about clean onboarding, predictable volume, and a commercial model that lets his business survive.

Why this role matters

Without suppliers, there's no service. Joe represents the fulfilment side: the actual kits, the actual labs, the actual results, the actual logistics. Every pattern in the catalogue depends on him being commercially viable enough to deliver it. The framework has three jobs from his perspective: make joining easy, make the work predictable, make the margin survive. If any of those fail, the supplier base shrinks, choice narrows, and the platform stops scaling.

Suppliers don't owe the NHS a service at a loss. They are commercial entities. The framework either makes the economics work for them or it doesn't. Pricing tiers, exclusivity, volume guarantees, exit terms: all of these have to add up to a margin Joe can run his business on. The framework's failure mode is the same as the commissioner's: too much bureaucracy, too little volume, suppliers walk.

What drives him

He started inside the NHS. Joe came up through NHS pathology in his twenties , a senior biomedical scientist for the best part of a decade before moving to the supplier side in his early thirties. He's seen the supply chain from both desks. The instinct to push back when an NHS commissioner asks for an SLA that can't actually be delivered comes from knowing what it looks like when the kit lands at the lab and the lab has been told something the lab can't do.

A pilot that nearly took the company under. A few years ago his team ran a high-profile NHS pilot that performed clinically , the kit worked, the lab results were clean, the return rates were strong. Commercially it died. The volumes promised in the procurement never materialised. The SLAs assumed a service shape the framework didn't fund. The team kept delivering long past the point the company should have walked away because no-one wanted to be the one who told the NHS the pilot was dead. The company nearly went under. Joe led the restructuring. He's careful now about which frameworks his company joins.

His team is dozens of people. When Joe pushes for clean onboarding and predictable volume, he's not making a procurement point , he's protecting payroll for a team that's been through one restructuring already. The framework either gets that or it doesn't. The HomeTest framing , transparent criteria, predictable volume, a framework that doesn't punish suppliers for being honest , is what makes him willing to join. If those things hold, his company stays. If they don't, his business survives by walking. He'd rather not walk.

What sits in Joe's territory

Joe is the fulfilment side of every configuration. Four things sit explicitly under his territory today, each one with live work attached.

Live supplier engagement state
Two Preliminary Market Engagement notices (PMEs) live. 32 organisations of mixed type and size have registered interest. Responses being collated to inform framework design. Supplier Forum 3 currently postponed, agenda being prepared for the rescheduled session. Ownership of Supplier Forum 3 now sits with the acute initiative team for the HIV / acute-use-case strand; commercial workstream stays involved for visibility.
Alpha and Beta call-off via the ACE Framework
The interim commercial route for Alpha and Private Beta. Call-off via SBS Lot 1 SBS10255. Retires when the formal framework lands. Not the steady-state position: it is the bridge while the framework is being built.
Supplier Onboarding Framework
Suppliers self-assess against published criteria, submit, then clear a single combined assurance gate (commercial, clinical, IG, technical, operational). Once admitted, the conformance certificate is portable across commissioners. Drafted as a Strawman in April 2026; process integration in flight. Affects the onboarding gateway Priya runs.
Royal Mail logistics interface
Carrier leg for kit dispatch and sample return. Volume discounts apply per shipper, not aggregated across NHS suppliers. NHSE engaged on a Health Samples package category. Fulfilment SLA under negotiation, including the requested NHS fast lane against Tracked 24 reliability gaps. NHSE acts as collective bargaining entity, not buyer. Affects every supplier's operational cost base.
Open Q for SLT: supplier off-boarding has no NHSE precedent
The framework needs a defined process for removing a supplier that fails standards, exits the market, or is replaced. There is no precedent elsewhere in NHSE to lift wholesale. PA23 provides the legal basis but operational mechanics (notice periods, evidence thresholds, appeals route, patient continuity, sample-in-flight handling) all need designing. Without it the framework locks in to first-mover suppliers by default.
Open Q for SLT: pricing structure (covered in the Future State team's Recommendation paper)
The supplier-preferred commercial structure surfaced through the RFI and supplier forums is a three-tier model: mobilisation fee plus activity plus volume floor. The activity tier is measured on cost per valid result (per completed test, loaded for repeat testing, sample rejection, postal failure, clinical review and abnormal-result escalation), not flat per-kit pricing. Flat per-kit pricing was rejected. Subscription was rejected at strategic level. The final position on the activity unit is covered in the Future State team's Recommendation paper. Substantiation depends on Health Economist input, currently blocked.

What Joe needs from the framework

Clean onboarding through a single gateway: he doesn't want to repeat clinical safety, IG, and technical assurance for every commissioner who buys. Predictable demand he can plan capacity around: not promises, but commissioning routes that aren't bottlenecked by 211 LAs each running their own procurement. A pricing structure that pays him on a three-tier basis (mobilisation, activity, volume floor), not flat per-kit and not against speculative volume. A clear exit if commercial viability collapses: not stuck with sunk integration costs.

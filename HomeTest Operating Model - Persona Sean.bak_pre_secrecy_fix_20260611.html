Sean.

The patient the appointment system fails · learning disability · every pathway at once
“He has had genuine real-world problems with attending medical appointments, understanding the advice, committing to the tests. HomeTest would have been a godsend.” — his brother, who works on this programme
Sean is not an archetype. He is a real man of 46 with learning disabilities and special needs, the brother of a member of the programme's commercial team, and his story is here with his family's blessing because it answers the question this whole model exists to answer: who does the current system quietly fail, and what does testing at home change for them? Abdul, Mark and Layla are the three Year-1 archetypes. Sean is the story that runs through all three — and through every pathway this model will ever carry.

The barrier stack

For Sean, the test was never the hard part. Everything before the test was. Attending: the journey to an unfamiliar building, the waiting room with its noise and strangers and unexplained delays, the appointment that starts whether or not he is ready. Understanding: advice given quickly, in clinical language, with no time to process and nothing to take away in a form that works for him. Committing: the gap between agreeing to a test in the room and the test actually happening, which the system records as his failure. A “did not attend” code does not distinguish would not from could not, and people like Sean accumulate them — and then the system, reading non-attendance as non-engagement, quietly deprioritises exactly the people who needed the most help to attend.

What HomeTest changes

  • The test comes to him. Home, with someone he trusts, at his own pace. No journey, no waiting room, no stranger with a needle on a schedule that isn't his.
  • Instructions that work for him. Easy-read formats, pictures and plain words, the chance to do it again tomorrow if today went wrong. ⚑ the programme's easy-read and accessible-format approach is to be documented.
  • A supported journey, not a solo one. A carer- or family-assisted flow where ordering, doing and understanding the test can involve the person who helps him — without taking the test away from him. ⚑ design approach to be documented.
  • Adjustments that travel. The NHS reasonable-adjustment flag exists so needs like Sean's are known wherever he turns up; the ordering step should read and honour it. ⚑ integration approach to be confirmed.
  • Results in his language. What the number means, what happens next, told plainly — to him, and with his consent to the person who supports him.

Why Sean is every pathway

Sean is not a CVKM story or a PSA story or an anaemia story. He is all of them, because the barrier stack is the same inside every pathway this model will ever run. Layla's page calls her journey “the access story”, and it is — for people whose barrier is time. Sean is the access story for people whose barrier is the system itself. People with learning disabilities are offered an annual health check that routinely includes bloods, and it is exactly at the venepuncture step that the journey most often stalls. And the curb-cut rule applies: a journey designed so Sean can complete it is a better journey for Abdul, Mark and Layla too — clearer instructions, calmer steps, fewer assumptions.

The wider lane. Sean leads what the portfolio calls the horizontal cohorts: learning disability, serious mental illness, needle-phobic patients, children and young people — the cohorts for whom every vertical pathway's hardest step is the same step. The senior sponsor asked in June 2026 for this stream to be documented; much of the good practice already exists in the system and needs championing rather than inventing. The portfolio-level lane lives on the DTx Operating Model; this page is its HomeTest anchor. ⚑ approach being documented.

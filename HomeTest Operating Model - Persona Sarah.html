The cast · meet Sarah

Sarah.

Commissioner
“I need this to save my budget, not strain it. Give me ready-made templates and a clear route to buy. I haven't got the headcount to bespoke this for every supplier.”
Director of Public Health at a local authority. Allocates the LA budget for sexual health and other commissioned services. Cares about saving money, hitting inequality targets, and making the service easy to roll out.

Why this role matters

Without commissioners, the platform has no buyer. Sarah is the demand side: whether she chooses to buy through the framework determines whether the model scales beyond two LAs and a clinical safety case. She has the budget. She has the political pressure (inequality targets, GP-pressure relief, value for money). She has the headcount problem: one team, dozens of services to commission, no time for bespoke per-supplier contracting. The framework either makes her job easier or it doesn't. If it doesn't, she'll buy bilaterally, the model fragments, and the centre's role evaporates.

The model scales when Sarah's buy decision gets simple. Pre-assured suppliers, published call-off terms, ready-made templates, single accounting route. Every step that adds bureaucracy to her side trades scale for control she didn't ask for. The framework's success metric is how many Sarahs use it without phoning their procurement lead first.

What Sarah needs from the framework

Three things, in order. First, suppliers she doesn't have to clinically assure herself: the conformance certificate has to be valid across patterns. Second, a buy mechanism that fits her existing commissioning calendar: call-off, not full procurement. Third, an exit route if a pattern stops working for her cohort: she will not lock in for five years on a platform she's never used.

Why local systems will actually contract

The model assumes local systems will draw down. The real question is what they get from doing so. Seven drivers sit in Sarah's territory: not aspirational, the things her finance and operations teams already track and account for.

  • Outpatient capacity released. Every test that doesn't need a clinic visit frees the slot for someone who does. Direct, measurable, and visible in elective recovery dashboards.
  • Reduced DNAs. A kit at home doesn't get missed because of work, transport, childcare or weather. The DNA cost (clinic time, reschedule cost, downstream risk) drops.
  • Workforce shortages eased. Especially phlebotomy. Capillary draw and self-sampling routes shift the bottleneck off scarce roles.
  • Elective recovery contribution. Faster pre-op diagnostic loops, faster surveillance loops, fewer in-clinic touchpoints. Counts toward national elective recovery commitments.
  • Diagnostics backlog reduction. Routine monitoring tests don't compete with diagnostic-pathway tests for lab capacity if the model routes them appropriately.
  • Patient demand. Patients increasingly expect digital-first. Local systems that can offer it look modern; those that can't look behind.
  • Tariff reform readiness. Tariff is moving toward outcome-based and capacity-released models. Systems that can show home-test contribution to capacity are positioned for the next pricing cycle.
Targeting and reach as a platform offer. The platform can broker targeting and outreach capabilities the commissioner cannot easily replicate locally: UKHSA data overlays, NHSE social media reach, geo-targeted channels like Nextdoor, and LSOA-level segmentation. This shifts the platform from a passive pipe to an active reach engine on the commissioner's behalf. Direction set; the exact operating shape and what sits inside the platform versus alongside it is being worked through.

Value proposition is different per audience. An LA Public Health commissioner cares about inequality reach. An acute trust commissioner cares about elective capacity. An ICB commissioner cares about pathway integration. The framework has to articulate the value sharply per customer, not as a single message. Currently no single owner pushing the per-customer narrative.

The commissioner pitch in 60 seconds

The pitch Sarah hears in the first ten minutes, before she decides whether the conversation is worth continuing. Direct answers to the questions she will ask. No aspiration, no architecture, no abstractions.

Where do I save money? Clinic slots released (capacity goes to elective recovery), reduced DNAs (kit at home doesn't miss appointments), phlebotomy workload eased (capillary draw replaces venepuncture for routine monitoring), and downstream diagnostic-pathway protection (routine tests stop competing with diagnostic tests for lab capacity). The cashable saving depends on the local pathway; the released capacity counts toward national elective recovery commitments regardless.
Who's going to pay me? Not the centre. Local systems retain commissioning budgets and contract locally. The platform takes a small percent fee per transaction per transaction to sustain itself; it does not hold the commissioner contract or the patient budget. Best-practice tariffs at pathway level are how the cost / benefit dislocation between pathology, phlebotomy and primary care gets closed. This is unresolved at SLT level and on the open governance questions list.
How quickly can I get this in front of patients? Suppliers are already accredited centrally. Templates are ready (DSA, DPIA, privacy notice, clinical safety starter). The commissioner self-assesses fit, signs the call-off, and goes live. Bespoke procurement is not on the path. The lead time is days for the buy mechanism; clinical pathway changes take longer and depend on local Trust readiness.
What if it doesn't work for my cohort? Exit route is part of the call-off terms. No five-year platform lock-in. Patterns are designed to be picked up and put down as the local need changes. Switching supplier within a pattern is also supported via the supplier-switching mechanism, not by tearing up the deployment.
What if I'm not an LA? The same shape works for an acute trust (Mark's PSA-AS pattern), a primary care setting (Layla's HbA1c pattern) or an ICB. The commissioner role is the same; the budget line and the patient cohort differ. Each commissioner type sees the value differently: LA cares about inequality reach, acute trust cares about elective capacity, ICB cares about pathway integration. The pitch needs to be sharpened per customer; that work has not yet been done.
